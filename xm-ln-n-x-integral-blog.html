Solving the Integral of \(x^m \ln^n(x)\)

By Prateek Chandra Jha | February 2025

While reviewing my old mathematics textbooks last weekend, I stumbled upon a fascinating integral that perfectly illustrates the beauty of change of variables techniques in calculus. The integral \(\int_0^1 x^m \ln^n(x) \, dx\) appears in various areas of mathematics, from statistical mechanics to complex analysis. Today, I'd like to share the elegant solution to this integral, demonstrating how successive substitutions can transform seemingly complex problems into familiar forms.

The Integral in Question

The integral we'll solve is:

\[ \int_0^1 x^m \ln^n(x) \, dx \]

where \(m\) and \(n\) are constants, with \(n\) being a non-negative integer. This integral combines two key functions—a power function \(x^m\) and a logarithmic function \(\ln^n(x)\)—creating an interesting analytical challenge.

Key Insight

The key to solving this integral is to transform it into a form involving the gamma function \(\Gamma(z)\). This approach leverages the powerful connection between the gamma function and improper integrals of the form \(\int_0^{\infty} t^{q-1}e^{-t} \, dt\).

Solution Using Change of Variables

We start by making our first substitution:

\[ u = -\ln(x) \]

This implies:

\[ \ln(x) = -u \] \[ x = e^{-u} \] \[ dx = -e^{-u} \, du \]

When \(x = 1\), \(u = 0\), and when \(x = 0\), \(u = \infty\).

Substituting these expressions into our original integral:

\begin{align} \int_0^1 x^m \ln^n(x) \, dx &= \int_{\infty}^0 (e^{-u})^m (-u)^n (-e^{-u} \, du) \\ &= \int_{\infty}^0 e^{-um} (-u)^n (-e^{-u} \, du) \\ &= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-um} (-u)^n (e^{-u} \, du) \\ &= (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} u^n e^{-(m+1)u} \, du \end{align}

We make a second substitution:

\[ t = (m+1)u \]

Which gives us:

\[ u = \frac{t}{m+1} \] \[ du = \frac{dt}{m+1} \]

Applying this second substitution:

\begin{align} (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} u^n e^{-(m+1)u} \, du &= (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} \left(\frac{t}{m+1}\right)^n e^{-t} \frac{dt}{m+1} \\ &= (-1)^n \int_0^{\infty} \frac{t^n}{(m+1)^n} e^{-t} \frac{dt}{m+1} \\ &= \frac{(-1)^n}{(m+1)^{n+1}} \int_0^{\infty} t^n e^{-t} \, dt \end{align}

We recognize that the integral \(\int_0^{\infty} t^{q-1} e^{-t} \, dt\) is the gamma function \(\Gamma(q)\). In our case, \(q-1 = n\), so \(q = n+1\):

\begin{align} \frac{(-1)^n}{(m+1)^{n+1}} \int_0^{\infty} t^n e^{-t} \, dt &= \frac{(-1)^n}{(m+1)^{n+1}} \Gamma(n+1) \end{align}

Since \(\Gamma(n+1) = n!\) for non-negative integers \(n\), we get our final result:

\begin{align} \int_0^1 x^m \ln^n(x) \, dx &= \frac{(-1)^n n!}{(m+1)^{n+1}} \end{align}
\[ \int_0^1 x^m \ln^n(x) \, dx = \frac{(-1)^n n!}{(m+1)^{n+1}} \]

Understanding the Solution

The solution to this integral reveals several interesting mathematical properties:

  1. The sign alternates with \(n\): Due to the \((-1)^n\) factor, the integral's sign alternates depending on whether \(n\) is even or odd.
  2. Factorial growth: The presence of \(n!\) means the integral's magnitude grows rapidly as \(n\) increases.
  3. Inverse relationship with \(m\): As \(m\) increases, the integral's magnitude decreases, following an inverse power law.

Special Cases

Case 1: \(n = 0\)

When \(n = 0\), our formula simplifies to:

\[ \int_0^1 x^m \, dx = \frac{1}{m+1} \]

This is indeed correct, as the integral evaluates to \(\left[\frac{x^{m+1}}{m+1}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{m+1}\).

Case 2: \(n = 1\)

When \(n = 1\), we get:

\[ \int_0^1 x^m \ln(x) \, dx = \frac{-1!}{(m+1)^2} = \frac{-1}{(m+1)^2} \]

Case 3: \(n = 2\)

When \(n = 2\), we get:

\[ \int_0^1 x^m \ln^2(x) \, dx = \frac{(-1)^2 \cdot 2!}{(m+1)^3} = \frac{2}{(m+1)^3} \]

Connections to the Gamma Function

The gamma function \(\Gamma(z)\) is a fundamental special function that extends the factorial to complex numbers. It is defined as:

\[ \Gamma(z) = \int_0^{\infty} t^{z-1} e^{-t} \, dt \]

For positive integers \(n\), we have \(\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!\), which means \(\Gamma(n+1) = n!\).

Our solution leverages this connection to transform a complex integral involving logarithms into a standardized form involving the gamma function.

Applications

This integral appears in various areas of mathematics and physics:

  • Statistical Mechanics: When calculating certain thermodynamic properties
  • Probability Theory: In the computation of moments for certain probability distributions
  • Mathematical Analysis: As a standard example in the theory of special functions
  • Numerical Integration: As a test case for numerical methods
